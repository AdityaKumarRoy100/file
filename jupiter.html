JUPITER

Jupiter, the largest planet in our solar system, is a gas giant with a mass more than two and a half times that of all other planets combined. Its atmosphere is primarily hydrogen and helium, creating colorful bands and swirls, including the Great Red Spot, a storm that has been raging for at least 400 years. Jupiter's rapid rotation (a day lasts only about 10 hours) causes it to bulge at the equator and flatten at the poles. The planet has a faint ring system and at least 79 known moons, including the four large Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Io is the most volcanically active body in the solar system, while Europa may harbor a subsurface ocean that could potentially support life. Jupiter's intense magnetic field, the strongest of any planet, creates powerful radiation belts. The planet's immense gravity has helped shape the solar system, sometimes deflecting comets and asteroids from Earth. Despite its size, Jupiter has a relatively low density, and if placed in a giant bathtub, it would float.
